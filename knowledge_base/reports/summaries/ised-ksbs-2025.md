# ISED Key Small Business Statistics 2025

## TL;DR
Canada's small business sector remains dominant (98.2% of 1.10M employer businesses), but employment growth has stalled with only 6,939 net new private sector jobs in 2024. Female entrepreneurs start businesses at higher rates but face lower long-term survival, highlighting a retention and scaling gap.

## Key Data Points
1. **Total employer businesses** — 1.10 million (98.2% small, 1.5% medium, 0.3% large)
2. **Small business employment** — 5.8 million workers (46.6% of private labour force)
3. **Net private sector employment change 2023→2024** — +6,939 (+0.1%, near-zero growth)
4. **Average annual business creation 2017–2021** — 81,030 per year
5. **Female-owned business birth rate** — 11.5% (vs. 9.3% male-owned)
6. **Female-owned business 16-year survival rate** — 41.3% (vs. 46.5% male-owned)
7. **SME share of goods exports** — 37.9% of $712.8B
8. **High-growth firm concentration** — Highest in information/cultural industries (7.5%)
9. **Quebec's share of Canadian private sector employment** — 22.1% (second only to Ontario at 39.7%)
10. **Quebec small employer businesses** — 228,622 as of December 2024

## Quebec-Specific Findings
- Quebec accounts for 22.1% of Canadian private sector employment, a significant economic engine second only to Ontario
- Quebec had 228,622 small employer businesses as of December 2024
- Quebec's employment profile aligns with national trends of stalled growth and challenges scaling female-founded enterprises

## Methodology & Limitations
- Data current to December 2024
- Full report accessed as free PDF
- Statistics compiled from multiple Canadian business registries and employment surveys
- Data on business creation rates (2017–2021 average) may reflect pre-pandemic normalcy

## Relevance to Our Work
- **Q5.1** (VC per capita): Only 46.6% of private labour force employed by small businesses; stalled employment growth (+0.1%) suggests macro headwinds constraining scaleup hiring
- **Q3.1** (talent pipeline): Female entrepreneurs' higher birth rate (11.5% vs. 9.3%) but lower survival rates (41.3% vs. 46.5%) indicate talent and capital access gaps in scaling
- **Q1.1** (startup formation): Average annual business creation of 81,030 firms; high-growth concentration in information/cultural industries suggests sectoral variation in scaling potential
